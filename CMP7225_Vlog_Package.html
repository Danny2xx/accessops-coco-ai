Section 1

Audit Executive Summary

For a Non-Technical Audience

This MSc project builds an AI system that automatically writes text descriptions for images — a real accessibility problem affecting 2.2 billion people with vision impairment worldwide. The student progressed from simple keyword-spotting tools all the way to a neural sequence generation pipeline, tested it on 2,500 images from Microsoft's standard benchmark, experimented with reinforcement learning to improve it, and built a working web application that routes predictions to human reviewers when the model is uncertain. The project is thorough, honest about what worked and what didn't, and demonstrates the full engineering and scientific process.

For a Technical Examiner

The project implements a complete image captioning pipeline on MS COCO 2017: word-level tokenisation (30k vocab, max_len=30), MobileNetV2 encoder with staged frozen→finetune transfer learning, LSTM decoder with teacher forcing, AdamW optimizer ablation (3 runs), beam search decoding ablation (4 strategies), SCST reinforcement learning (1 epoch, correctly implemented but regressed due to reward sparsity and insufficient training budget), RAG retrieval augmentation (566k caption corpus, Jaccard-gated, marginal −0.001 delta), and a confidence-threshold human reroute policy (threshold=0.511, 50/50 split, auto-subset BLEU-4=0.281). Best BLEU-4: 0.2473 (beam k=3, λ=0.6), competitive with Show Attend Tell (0.24). Full ablation study across all stages. Deployed FastAPI + React webapp with live inference.

The CNN performed worse: micro-F1 of 0.355 versus 0.458 for the MLP. The reason: MobileNetV2 uses global average pooling internally, which collapses the spatial dimensions. By the time features reach my classifier, there's no spatial structure left for a CNN to exploit. 2:30–4:00 "The captioning model is an encoder-decoder: MobileNetV2 encodes the image, an LSTM decoder generates the caption word by word using teacher forcing. Starting from scratch — no pretrained weights, 192×192 input — BLEU-4 was 0.169 on 2,500 test images. BLEU-4 measures 4-gram overlap with human references. Then transfer learning: froze the MobileNetV2 encoder, trained only the decoder, then unfroze for fine-tuning. I also increased resolution from 192 to 224, matching MobileNetV2's native input size. Result: BLEU-4 jumped to 0.219 — a 30% relative improvement. The two-phase approach prevents catastrophic forgetting: freezing the encoder early means the pretrained ImageNet features aren't destroyed by noisy decoder gradients during early training." artifacts/stage3/training_curve_loss.png then artifacts/stage4/training_curve_loss.png. 4:00–5:30 "From the Stage 4 checkpoint, I ran an optimiser ablation: Adam at 1e-4, AdamW at 8e-5, and AdamW at 5e-5. The winner was AdamW with LR=5e-5, achieving validation loss of 2.422 compared to Adam's 2.438. AdamW uses decoupled weight decay — it regularises model weights separately from the gradient update. This matters for fine-tuning because it prevents the model drifting too far from pretrained weights. Then decoding strategies. Greedy picks the most probable word at each step. Beam search maintains 3 candidate sequences and scores by length-normalised log-probability. Beam k=3 with length penalty 0.6 improved BLEU-4 from 0.231 to 0.247 — a 7% further improvement. Beam=5 actually performed worse than beam=3 — at this model size, diversity beyond 3 candidates adds noise rather than quality." artifacts/stage8_ablation/figures/chart_ablation_train_optimizer_bleu4.png then chart_ablation_decode_bleu4.png. "Systematic" optimisation with critical insight.
5:30–6:30 "I also attempted a cross-attention architecture — replacing simple image-feature concat with multi-head attention over the 7×7 spatial feature map. The attention model's BLEU-4 was 0.0001 — essentially zero. The cause: Phase 1 training was interrupted at epoch 3 of 6 due to compute constraints — each epoch took roughly 2.5 hours on my M4 MacBook. The attention weights were undertrained when I moved to fine-tuning, and the validation loss spiked from 4.5 to 5.3 in Phase 2. Partially-trained attention destabilised rather than improved the model. This demonstrates an important principle: attention mechanisms require more training epochs than simple concat approaches, and insufficient warm-up leads to degenerate weights." Stage 4D training curve showing val_loss spike in Phase 2. Callout: "Val loss: 4.48 → 5.17 at Phase 2 epoch 1" LO2 Honest failure analysis with mechanical explanation. "Critical evaluation" means not hiding failures — explaining them rigorously. Examiners respect this far more than omitting it.
6:30–7:45 "Stage 6 was reinforcement learning — Self-Critical Sequence Training from Rennie et al. 2017. The model generates two captions: one sampled stochastically, one greedy decode as baseline. The reward is the difference in their BLEU-4 scores. The model learns to push probability mass toward captions that score better than its own greedy baseline. After one epoch of SCST, BLEU-4 was 0.222. The greedy Stage 5 baseline was also 0.222. RL did not improve performance. This is a negative result — but an honest one with clear analysis. Four factors: published SCST training uses 30–50 epochs, I ran one. BLEU-4 is a sparse reward — many captions have zero 4-gram overlap, producing zero reward and high gradient variance. The original paper uses CIDEr, which is smoother. Single-reference BLEU adds noise — COCO has 5 captions per image; I used one. And the learning rate was likely too high, risking catastrophic forgetting. This result is consistent with the literature." SCST algorithm diagram (simple: sample → greedy → reward delta). At a threshold of 0.511, the top 50% most confident predictions auto-publish — these achieve BLEU-4 of 0.281, 27% above the overall average. The bottom 50% go to a human reviewer. On the 8.3% of images where retrieval triggered, 62.8% of cases improved. But full-set BLEU-4 delta was −0.001. Likely Examiner Questions & Model Answers

QuestionModel Answer
"Why did you choose MobileNetV2?" MobileNetV2 is a well-established lightweight backbone pretrained on ImageNet. It provides strong representations at low computational cost — each epoch on the full COCO training set takes ~2.5 hours on my hardware. A ViT or ResNet50 would extract richer features but require significantly more compute. MobileNetV2 was the appropriate choice given the compute budget.
"BLEU-4 is a known weak metric — why use it?" BLEU-4 is the standard benchmark metric for MS COCO, making results directly comparable to published baselines (Show and Tell 0.27, Show Attend Tell 0.24). I acknowledge its limitations — it doesn't capture semantic similarity, penalises valid paraphrases, and is corpus-level. In a production system I'd complement it with CIDEr and human evaluation. For research comparison, BLEU-4 enables fair benchmarking.
"Why did your RL implementation fail?" Four reasons, documented in the analysis: insufficient training epochs (1 vs 30–50 published), BLEU-4 reward sparsity (many captions score zero reward, creating high-variance gradients), single-reference noise (COCO has 5 references per image; I used one), and potentially too-high learning rate. The original SCST paper uses CIDEr, specifically designed to be smooth for captioning. This is a known challenge in RL for sequence generation.
"What would you do differently with more compute?" Three things: retrain Stage 4D cross-attention with complete Phase 1 (6 full epochs before fine-tuning); run SCST for 10–15 epochs with CIDEr reward and multi-reference evaluation; and evaluate on a held-out domain (medical images) to measure generalisation.
"Why is the full-set BLEU-4 lower than the fast-subset?" The fast subset was evaluated with beam search on 500 images (BLEU-4=0.247). The full-set used greedy decoding on 2,500 images (0.222). They're not directly comparable — different decode strategy and different sample size. The honest full-to-full comparison is Stage 3 scratch (0.169) vs Stage 5 full-eval (0.222), a 32% relative improvement.
"How did you select threshold 0.511?" Through a principled sweep across 7 threshold values from 0.434 to 0.594, covering 20% to 80% auto-publish rates. Each operating point was evaluated on BLEU-4 of the auto-published subset. I selected the balanced 50/50 operating point as it represents equal split of effort between automation and human review, while the auto-published subset still achieves BLEU-4=0.281, 27% above the overall mean.
"Why didn't RAG improve performance?" RAG degraded full-set BLEU-4 by 0.001. Two explanations: the gate conditions (sim≥0.6 AND conf<0.4) only trigger for 8.3% of images, so improvement on triggered cases is diluted. For triggered cases, 62.8% improved — the issue is the gate being too conservative for wider impact. A lower similarity threshold or embedding-based retrieval would likely improve coverage.
"What is the confidence score based on?" sigmoid(mean_log_prob − 1.0), where mean_log_prob is the length-penalised average log-probability of the beam search output. The −1.0 offset centres the sigmoid so typical beam-search log-probs map to a useful [0,1] range. It's a calibrated proxy for model uncertainty, not a frequentist probability.
"You said your result beats Show Attend Tell — isn't that unfair?" It's a reasonable comparison on the same benchmark. Both use BLEU-4 on COCO test images. The caveat is that Show Attend Tell was published in 2015 on a different COCO split — direct numerical comparison has caveats, which I acknowledge. The point is that a simpler architecture, well-optimised, can reach the vicinity of attention-based models from that era.
